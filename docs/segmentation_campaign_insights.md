# Customer Segmentation, Campaign Analysis & Customer Journey
**Marketing & Insight Analyst Portfolio | Building Materials Distributor (sample data)**

---

## 1. Customer Segmentation — RFM Model

Using Recency (days since last order), Frequency (lifetime order count), and
Monetary (lifetime spend), customers fall into four segments:

| Segment | Definition | Commercial Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| **Champion** | Ordered in last 90 days, 10+ lifetime orders | Highest-value, most active accounts — protect and reward these |
| **Loyal** | Ordered in last 180 days, 5+ lifetime orders | Reliable base, not yet maximised — good upsell target |
| **Developing** | Everyone else with at least 1 order | New or infrequent — needs nurturing toward Loyal |
| **At Risk / Dormant** | No order in 365+ days | Was active once, has gone quiet — win-back candidate |

**Key insight:** Champions are a small share of the customer base but
disproportionately drive revenue — a classic 80/20 pattern. The commercial
priority isn't acquisition volume, it's **protecting Champions from churn**
and **converting Loyal customers into Champions** through targeted account
management rather than generic campaigns.

**Recommendation:** Champions shouldn't receive the same blanket promotional
emails as Retail customers — a named account manager check-in and early
access to stock/pricing is more appropriate than a discount code, which risks
giving away margin on purchases that would have happened anyway.

---

## 2. Campaign Channel Effectiveness

- **Direct Mail outperformed digital channels on ROI** in this sample — counter
  to the common assumption that digital is always cheaper/better. For a trade
  and contractor audience, a physical mailer may cut through more effectively
  than an email competing for attention in a busy inbox.
- **Email and SMS campaigns with zero conversions** show -100% ROI, which more
  likely reflects a targeting/offer mismatch than a channel-level problem —
  worth investigating whether the offer was timed against something contractors
  actually needed.

**Recommendation:** Before scaling any single channel, run a controlled test
(same message, same segment, across Email/SMS/Direct Mail on a matched sample)
rather than carrying forward last quarter's channel mix by default.

## 3. Customer Journey Mapping — New Trade Account Onboarding

| Stage | Timing | Opportunity |
|---|---|---|
| **Signup** | Day 0 | Welcome series — set expectations on trade pricing, account manager contact, delivery lead times |
| **First order** | Week 1–4 | Highest-risk drop-off point — a poor first-order experience (wrong delivery, confusing invoice) can prevent a return visit entirely |
| **Repeat purchase window** | Month 2–4 | Where "Developing" segment customers either progress to "Loyal" or go quiet — a light-touch check-in can meaningfully lift retention |
| **Loyalty eligibility** | Month 6+ | Natural point to introduce a loyalty programme — introducing it earlier risks it feeling like a generic discount rather than an earned reward |
| **At-risk trigger** | 180+ days since last order | Automated re-engagement referencing past purchase categories, rather than a generic "we miss you" message |

**Recommendation:** Build a manual or semi-automated quality check on
first orders above a defined value — a low-cost intervention at the
highest-leverage point in the customer journey.

---

## Summary

1. Revenue is concentrated in a small Champion segment — retention here
   matters more than acquisition volume.
2. Direct Mail is outperforming digital on ROI for this audience — worth
   testing further rather than defaulting to email-first.
3. The first-order experience is the highest-risk moment in the customer
   journey and the cheapest place to intervene.
